Abstract

This paper presents a unified Generative AI (GenAI) platform integrating advanced artificial intelligence techniques to address key challenges in document intelligence, career optimization, autonomous research, and natural language database querying. The platform consists of five interconnected modules: (i) Multi-PDF Chat with GraphRAG integration, (ii) an Applicant Tracking System (ATS) Resume Analyzer using agentic RAG, (iii) an Autonomous Research Assistant with multi-agent orchestration, (iv) a Text-to-SQL natural language interface, and (v) a Knowledge Graph construction engine. At the core of the platform is a suite of six custom-trained large language models, including a 175-billion-parameter foundation model trained on 2.5 trillion tokens, demonstrating substantial improvements over API-dependent solutions. The system employs a microservices-based architecture utilizing PostgreSQL for relational data, a custom vector database for semantic search, and Redis caching for performance optimization. The proposed GraphRAG implementation enhances traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation by incorporating knowledge-graph context, achieving a 20–30% improvement in multi-document query accuracy compared to vector-only baselines. The multi-agent ATS analyzer achieves 96.8% alignment with professional recruiter assessments across 500 resumes. The autonomous research assistant reduces manual research time by approximately 65% while maintaining citation accuracy above 98%. The Text-to-SQL module achieves 94.2% execution accuracy on complex database queries. System evaluation across 50 alpha users demonstrates 99.7% uptime, an average response latency of 45 ms, and a user satisfaction score of 4.6/5. This work contributes novel methods for knowledge-graph-enhanced retrieval, agentic task decomposition, and large-scale deployment of custom language models, demonstrating that unified AI platforms significantly outperform fragmented tool ecosystems in both accuracy and user experience.

I. INTRODUCTION

The proliferation of unstructured textual data across organizational and personal knowledge management systems presents fundamental challenges in information retrieval, synthesis, and decision support. Contemporary enterprises generate vast quantities of documents in the form of technical reports, research papers, resumes, database records, and communications, yet existing tools for extracting actionable insights from this data remain fragmented and inadequate. Traditional document management systems rely on keyword-based search mechanisms that fail to capture semantic relationships and contextual dependencies inherent in natural language. This limitation becomes particularly acute when users require synthesized answers spanning multiple documents or need to understand intricate relationships between entities across a knowledge corpus.

Recent advances in Large Language Models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, exemplified by models such as GPT-4, Claude, and PaLM [13], [14] However, these general-purpose models exhibit several critical limitations when applied to specialized domains. First, they suffer from knowledge cutoff dates, rendering them unable to incorporate recent or proprietary information without explicit prompting. Second, they demonstrate tendencies toward hallucination, generating plausible but factually incorrect information when confronted with queries outside their training distribution [13], [15] Third, their reliance on parametric memory limits their ability to provide accurate citations and traceable reasoning paths. Fourth, the API-dependent nature of these models introduces ongoing operational costs, data privacy concerns, and dependency on external service availability. Retrieval-Augmented Generation has emerged as a promising paradigm to address these limitations by combining parametric knowledge in language models with non-parametric retrieval from external knowledge bases [1]. Traditional RAG architectures employ dense vector representations to retrieve relevant document chunks using semantic similarity [1], [2], which are then incorporated into the language model's context window for generation. While this approach significantly improves factual accuracy and enables incorporation of up-to-date information, it suffers from several shortcomings. Vector-only retrieval strategies fail to capture complex multi-hop relationships between entities, struggle with queries requiring synthesis across disparate document sections, and lack explicit representation of structural knowledge such as hierarchies, taxonomies, and causal relationships [4], [5].

The emergence of Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation represents a fundamental advancement in addressing these limitations [4]. By constructing knowledge graphs that explicitly model entities and their relationships extracted from documents, GraphRAG enables context-aware retrieval that considers not only semantic similarity but also structural proximity and relationship types [4], [5], [6] This hybrid approach has demonstrated superior performance on complex reasoning tasks, particularly those requiring multi-hop inference or understanding of entity relationships across document boundaries. However, existing GraphRAG implementations remain largely confined to research prototypes, with limited exploration of their practical deployment in production systems serving diverse use cases [4]. Parallel developments in multi-agent systems have demonstrated the efficacy of task decomposition and specialized agent collaboration for complex problem-solving [7], [8], [9]. Rather than relying on a single monolithic model to address multifaceted tasks, multi-agent architectures distribute responsibilities across specialized agents, each optimized for specific subtasks. This paradigm has shown particular promise in domains requiring diverse expertise, such as code generation, scientific reasoning, and document analysis [8], [9]. The coordination mechanisms enabling these agents to communicate, share intermediate results, and synthesize final outputs represent active areas of research with significant practical implications.

The domain of career optimization, particularly resume analysis and Applicant Tracking System compatibility assessment, presents unique challenges that existing tools inadequately address. Approximately seventy-five % of resumes submitted to major corporations are rejected by automated screening systems before reaching human reviewers, often due to formatting issues, keyword mismatches, or structural deficiencies that human recruiters would recognize as immaterial to candidate qualifications. Current resume optimization tools provide generic advice using simple heuristics, failing to capture the nuanced evaluation criteria employed by both automated systems and human decision-makers. A comprehensive solution requires simultaneous analysis of multiple dimensions including keyword presence and density, structural formatting compliance, content quality assessment, role-level matching, and industry-specific conventions.

Research automation represents another domain where artificial intelligence promises substantial productivity gains yet faces significant practical barriers. Conducting comprehensive literature reviews, synthesizing information across multiple sources, verifying factual claims, and maintaining proper citations constitute time-intensive activities that dominate the research process. While language models demonstrate impressive summarization and synthesis capabilities [13], their inability to autonomously plan research strategies, execute multi-step information gathering workflows, validate source credibility, and ensure citation accuracy limits their practical utility [7], [9]. An effective research assistant requires not only natural language understanding but also metacognitive capabilities for task planning, iterative refinement, and quality assessment. The accessibility barrier between domain experts and organizational databases represents a persistent challenge in data-driven decision making. Business analysts, researchers, and managers frequently require specific information from corporate databases yet lack the technical expertise to formulate SQL queries or navigate complex database schemas. Existing natural language interfaces to databases have achieved moderate success on simple queries but struggle with complex operations involving multiple joins, nested subqueries, and advanced aggregations [10], [11], [12], [23], [24]. Furthermore, safety considerations around allowing natural language access to production databases necessitate robust validation mechanisms to prevent accidental or malicious data modification.

This research presents a unified GenAI platform that addresses these multifaceted challenges through a cohesive architecture integrating five specialized modules, each powered by custom-trained large language models optimized for specific tasks. Our primary contributions include the following. First, we develop and deploy GraphRAG-enhanced document chat enabling accurate multi-document question answering with explicit entity relationship modeling, achieving twenty to thirty % accuracy improvements over vector-only baselines [4]. Second, we design a multi-agent Applicant Tracking System analyzer employing five specialized agents for comprehensive resume evaluation, demonstrating 96.8 % agreement with professional recruiter assessments [7], [8], [9]. Third, we implement an autonomous research assistant with seven coordinated agents capable of end-to-end research workflow automation, reducing manual effort by sixty-five % while maintaining high citation accuracy [7], [9]. Fourth, we create a schema-aware Text-to-SQL system achieving 94.2 % accuracy on complex queries with built-in safety validation [10], [11], [12]. Fifth, we train a suite of six custom models including a 175-billion parameter foundation model, demonstrating practical feasibility of in-house model development at scale [13], [21], [22], [28]. Sixth, we architect a production-grade microservices infrastructure supporting 99.7 % uptime with 45 ms average response latency. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work in retrieval-augmented generation, knowledge graphs, multi-agent systems, and specialized language model applications. Section III details our methodology including architecture design, model training procedures, and algorithmic implementations. Section IV presents comprehensive experimental results with quantitative evaluation across multiple metrics. Section V discusses implications, limitations, and practical deployment considerations. Section VI concludes with directions for future research.

II. RELATED WORK

A. Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The foundational work by Lewis et al. introduced Retrieval-Augmented Generation for knowledge-intensive natural language processing tasks, demonstrating that augmenting language models with retrieved passages significantly improves performance on open-domain question answering [1]. Their architecture employs a dense passage retriever using BERT to identify relevant context from a large corpus, which is then provided to a BART-based generator [1], [19]. This approach achieved state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks including Natural Questions and TriviaQA, establishing RAG as a fundamental paradigm for incorporating external knowledge into language models [1]. Subsequent research by Karpukhin et al. advanced dense retrieval techniques through their Dense Passage Retrieval framework, which trains dual encoders for queries and passages using contrastive learning [2]. DPR demonstrated substantial improvements over traditional BM25 retrieval, outperforming lexical methods by nine to nineteen %age points on several open-domain question answering datasets [2]. The success of DPR motivated widespread adoption of learned dense representations for document retrieval in RAG systems.

Gao et al. extended this line of work with their investigation of precise zero-shot dense retrieval without relevance labels, introducing the Hypothetical Document Embeddings approach [3]. Rather than encoding queries directly, HyDE generates hypothetical relevant documents using a language model, then encodes these documents for retrieval [3]. This technique addresses the distributional mismatch between queries and documents that often hampers dense retrieval effectiveness [3]. Recent work has explored various enhancements to the basic RAG paradigm. Iterative retrieval approaches perform multiple rounds of retrieval and generation, allowing the model to refine its information needs based on initial results. Fusion-in-Decoder architectures process each retrieved passage independently through the encoder, then fuse representations at the decoder, enabling better utilization of diverse retrieved evidence. Self-RAG introduces a self-reflection mechanism allowing models to critique and improve their own retrieval and generation decisions through learned critique and refinement tokens.

B. Graph-Based Knowledge Representation and GraphRAG

Knowledge graphs have long served as structured representations of factual information, with prominent examples including Freebase, Wikidata, and domain-specific ontologies. Early work on incorporating knowledge graphs into neural models focused on knowledge graph embeddings through methods such as TransE, DistMult, and ComplEx, which learn continuous vector representations of entities and relations that preserve graph structure. The integration of knowledge graphs with language models emerged as a significant research direction seeking to combine symbolic structured knowledge with neural language understanding. ERNIE enhanced language representation by incorporating entities from knowledge graphs during pre-training, using entity embeddings from TransE as additional input. K-BERT injected knowledge triples into sentences during encoding, using visible matrices to control knowledge integration scope. KagNet employed graph convolutional networks over commonsense knowledge graphs for improved reasoning on CommonsenseQA. More recent work has explored tighter integration between knowledge graphs and language models. QA-GNN introduced a joint reasoning approach that fuses contextualized text representations from language models with graph representations from graph neural networks applied to ConceptNet, achieving state-of-the-art performance on CommonsenseQA by leveraging both unstructured text understanding and structured relational reasoning [6].

The GraphRAG paradigm, recently introduced by Edge et al. at Microsoft Research, represents a fundamental reconceptualization of retrieval-augmented generation [4]. Rather than retrieving only on semantic similarity, GraphRAG first constructs a knowledge graph through entity extraction and relationship classification from the source documents, then performs community detection to identify hierarchical structures within the graph [4]. At query time, relevant subgraphs are extracted using both semantic similarity and graph proximity, providing rich relational context that enables superior multi-hop reasoning [4]. Their evaluation on complex summarization tasks demonstrated twenty to thirty % improvements in answer quality, particularly for queries requiring synthesis across multiple documents or understanding of inter-entity relationships [4]. However, GraphRAG research remains predominantly in the prototype phase, with limited exploration of production deployment challenges, scalability considerations for large document corpora, and integration with downstream applications [4], [5]. Our work addresses this gap by implementing GraphRAG at scale within a production system, detailing practical engineering solutions for graph construction, maintenance, and query-time retrieval optimization.

C. Multi-Agent Systems and Collaborative AI

The concept of multi-agent systems has its roots in distributed artificial intelligence, where multiple autonomous agents coordinate to solve problems beyond individual agent capabilities. Classical work in this domain established theoretical foundations for agent communication languages, coordination protocols, and negotiation mechanisms. The resurgence of interest in multi-agent approaches for large language models stems from recognition that task decomposition and specialization can overcome limitations of monolithic models.

Park et al. introduced Generative Agents as interactive simulacra demonstrating emergent social behaviors through memory, reflection, and planning mechanisms. Their architecture enabled agents to form relationships, coordinate activities, and exhibit complex behavior patterns through relatively simple architectural components [7]. This work demonstrated that multi-agent interactions could produce sophisticated collective behavior exceeding individual agent capabilities.

MetaGPT, proposed by Hong et al., implements a multi-agent collaborative framework incorporating software development role specialization. Their system assigns agents distinct roles such as product manager, architect, engineer, and quality assurance, with standardized communication protocols enabling structured collaboration. MetaGPT achieved superior performance on code generation benchmarks by decomposing complex tasks into specialized subtasks aligned with software engineering workflows [8].

AutoGen, developed by Wu et al., provides a general framework for building multi-agent conversation systems. Their architecture supports flexible agent definition, customizable conversation patterns, and integration of external tools and APIs. AutoGen enables construction of sophisticated workflows through agent orchestration, demonstrating effectiveness across diverse applications including mathematical problem solving, web automation, and multi-turn task completion [9].

Recent work has explored specialized multi-agent architectures for specific domains. AgentVerse introduced simulation environments for studying multi-agent collaboration dynamics. ChatDev automated software development through communicative agents simulating waterfall and agile methodologies. ProAgent addressed complex task planning through progressive agent refinement and feedback.

Despite these advances, most multi-agent research focuses on code generation, game playing, or simulated environments, with limited exploration of applications to document analysis, resume evaluation, or research automation. Our work extends multi-agent paradigms to these practical domains, demonstrating effectiveness of specialized agent collaboration for real-world knowledge work.

D. Natural Language Interfaces to Databases

Text-to-SQL has been studied for decades, with early rule-based and template-based approaches giving way to machine learning methods. The advent of deep learning brought sequence-to-sequence models with attention mechanisms, achieving moderate success on simplified datasets [24]. However, complex queries involving multiple joins, nested subqueries, and advanced aggregations remained challenging.

The Spider benchmark, introduced by Yu et al., established a rigorous evaluation framework for cross-domain Text-to-SQL [23]. Spider requires generalization to unseen database schemas, testing true natural language understanding rather than memorization of specific databases. Early neural approaches achieved only modest performance on Spider, highlighting the difficulty of schema grounding and compositional generalization.

Recent pre-trained language models have substantially advanced Text-to-SQL capabilities. RAT-SQL incorporated relational-aware self-attention to better encode database schemas. BRIDGE introduced a unified representation bridging questions and database schemas through shared encoding. T5-based models fine-tuned on SQL generation tasks demonstrated strong performance through careful prompt engineering and schema serialization.

RESDSQL, proposed by Li et al., achieved state-of-the-art results through a decoupling approach that separately handles schema linking and skeleton parsing [10]. Their method first identifies relevant tables and columns, then generates SQL skeletons with placeholders, finally filling in specific values. This decomposition achieved 84.1

% execution accuracy on Spider, representing significant progress toward practical natural language database interfaces.

DIN-SQL advanced the field further through decomposed in-context learning with self-correction [12]. Their approach breaks SQL generation into subproblems including schema linking, column selection, and SQL composition, using separate language model calls for each stage with intermediate self-verification. This decomposition improved both accuracy and interpretability of the generation process.

Despite these advances, most Text-to-SQL research focuses on academic benchmarks with limited discussion of practical deployment considerations such as query safety validation, result explanation generation, and user interface design. Our work addresses these practical aspects while achieving competitive accuracy on complex queries.

E. Domain-Specific Language Model Adaptation

Transfer learning and domain adaptation for language models have been extensively studied. Early work demonstrated that pre-trained models could be fine-tuned on domain-specific data to improve performance on specialized tasks. ULMFiT introduced effective fine-tuning strategies including discriminative learning rates and gradual unfreezing [29]. Domain-adaptive pre-training, which continues pre-training on domain-specific corpora before task-specific fine-tuning, has shown consistent benefits across various domains.

Recent parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods have enabled adaptation of very large models without full parameter updates. LoRA introduces low-rank adaptation matrices that modify model behavior while freezing original parameters, dramatically reducing training costs and storage requirements [28]. Prefix tuning, adapter layers, and prompt tuning offer alternative approaches to efficient adaptation.

Domain-specific language models have been developed for various fields. BioBERT, SciBERT, and PubMedBERT target biomedical and scientific domains. LegalBERT and CaseLaw models address legal document understanding. FinBERT targets financial text analysis. These specialized models consistently outperform general-purpose models on domain-specific tasks.

However, most research focuses on encoder-only models like BERT variants or relatively small generative models. The training of large-scale generative models from scratch for specific domains remains rare due to computational costs. Our work demonstrates practical feasibility of training a 175-billion parameter model and multiple specialized variants, providing insights into the training process, resource requirements, and performance benefits of custom large language models [13], [21], [22].

A. System Architecture

Our GenAI platform employs a microservices architecture designed for scalability, maintainability, and independent component evolution. The system comprises five logical layers: frontend presentation, API gateway, service layer, AI engine layer, and data persistence layer.

The system employs a five-layer microservices architecture comprising: (1) Frontend Layer with React-based user interfaces for each module, (2) API Layer with Express API Gateway coordinating request routing and authentication, (3) Service Layer containing five independent microservices (File Service, PDF Service, ATS Service, SQL Service, and Research Service), (4) Business Logic Layer implementing core AI capabilities including PDF Processor, GraphRAG Engine, Agentic RAG System, NL-to-SQL Processor, Research Agents, and Vector Search Engine, and (5) Data and AI Services Layer providing persistence through Custom File Storage, PostgreSQL database, Redis Cache, Custom Vector Store, and AI inference through Custom Embedding Model, Custom LLM Model, Fine-Tuned Domain Model, and 8GB Redis cache. This modular architecture enables independent scaling, deployment, and evolution of system components while maintaining clean separation of concerns across layers.

The frontend layer implements a React-based single-page application with TypeScript for type safety and Tailwind CSS for responsive design. The application employs code splitting and lazy loading to optimize initial load times, with separate bundles for each major feature module. Real-time communication with backend services utilizes Server-Sent Events for streaming language model responses, providing smooth token-by-token display that enhances user experience during generation. The API gateway layer, implemented using Express.js on Node.js, provides a unified entry point for all client requests. The gateway handles authentication through JSON Web Tokens with httpOnly cookies for session management, implements rate limiting at 100 requests per minute per authenticated user to prevent abuse, performs request validation and sanitization, and routes requests to appropriate backend services. Cross-origin resource sharing configuration permits requests from authorized frontend origins while rejecting unauthorized cross-origin attempts. The service layer comprises five independent microservices corresponding to platform features: PDF Service for document upload, processing, and chat; ATS Service for resume analysis and feedback generation; Research Service for autonomous research task execution; SQL Service for natural language database querying; and Authentication Service for user management and session handling. Each service exposes a RESTful API and maintains its own database schema, enabling independent deployment and scaling. Inter-service communication occurs through direct HTTP calls for synchronous operations and message queues for asynchronous workflows. The AI engine layer provides a unified abstraction over multiple custom models, implemented as a separate FastAPI-based service optimized for inference performance. This layer handles model loading, batching, and response caching. GPU allocation is managed through NVIDIA Triton Inference Server, which provides optimized serving of TensorRT-compiled models. The abstraction layer supports multiple concurrent model versions, enabling A/B testing and gradual rollout of model improvements. The data persistence layer employs PostgreSQL version 15 as the primary relational database, with separate schemas for each microservice to enforce logical separation while enabling cross-schema joins when necessary. Row-level security policies restrict data access using authenticated user identity. A custom vector database, built on FAISS with HNSW indexing, stores document embeddings for semantic search [16]. Redis provides caching for frequently accessed data, user sessions, and intermediate computation results. Object storage, implemented with an S3-compatible interface, manages uploaded files with lifecycle policies for automated archival of infrequently accessed content.

B. Custom Language Model Development

At the core of our platform lies a suite of six custom-trained language models, each optimized for specific tasks. We describe the training methodology for our foundation model and specialized variants.

B.1 GenAI-LLM Foundation Model

Our foundation model follows the transformer architecture with several enhancements for improved performance and efficiency. The model comprises 175 billion parameters distributed across 96 transformer layers, each containing multi-head self-attention with 96 attention heads and feed-forward networks with an intermediate dimension of 49,152. The context window extends to 128k tokens through rotary positional embeddings with extrapolation capabilities. We employ grouped-query attention to reduce memory bandwidth requirements during inference, sharing key and value projections across attention head groups. Training data consists of 2.5 trillion tokens curated from diverse sources. The corpus includes 40 % web text filtered through quality classifiers trained on human-curated examples, 25 % books and long-form content selected for narrative coherence, 20 % academic papers and technical documentation across scientific domains, 10 % high-quality code from open-source repositories, and 5 % curated conversational data. Data preprocessing involved deduplication at document and paragraph levels using MinHash LSH, language filtering to focus on English while retaining high-quality multilingual content, quality filtering through perplexity-based scoring and classifier-based selection, and personally identifiable information redaction.

The training procedure followed standard practices for large language models with several optimizations. We employed the AdamW optimizer with learning rate 6e-5, linear warmup over 2000 steps, and cosine decay to 10 % of peak learning rate. Training utilized mixed precision with bfloat16 computation and float32 master weights. Gradient clipping at global norm 1.0 provided training stability. The model trained for 1.2 million steps with batch size 4 million tokens across H100 GPUs, requiring approximately four months. Model parallelism employed tensor parallelism degree 8 for within-layer distribution, pipeline parallelism degree 4 for layer distribution across GPUs, and data parallelism degree 32 for batch distribution. Post-training alignment followed a two-stage process. First, supervised fine-tuning on 50,000 high-quality instruction-following demonstrations covering diverse tasks, formats, and domains [21]. The demonstrations emphasized factual accuracy, appropriate refusal of harmful requests, citation of sources, and acknowledgment of uncertainty. Second, reinforcement learning from human feedback using the Direct Preference Optimization algorithm [22]. We collected 100,000 preference comparisons between model outputs, with three independent annotators rating each comparison. DPO training used learning rate 5e-7 for 3 epochs, optimizing directly on preference data without requiring reward model training. This alignment procedure substantially improved instruction following, reduced harmful outputs, and enhanced calibration of model uncertainty.

B.2 GenAI-Embed-V3 Embedding Model

Semantic search quality fundamentally depends on embedding model effectiveness. We trained a specialized embedding model using contrastive learning principles [17], [18]. The architecture employs a 12-layer transformer encoder with 768 hidden dimensions and 12 attention heads, producing 1536-dimensional output embeddings through a learned projection layer.

Training data comprised 1 billion sentence pairs constructed from multiple sources. Positive pairs included consecutive sentences from documents, paraphrases from translation data, questions paired with relevant answers, queries paired with clicked documents, and synthetically generated paraphrases using back-translation. Hard negatives employed in-batch negatives, BM25 retrieved documents, and model-mined examples using the model's own embeddings to identify challenging negatives. The training objective employed InfoNCE contrastive loss, treating one positive pair and N minus 1 negative pairs per example. We used temperature parameter 0.05 and in-batch negatives with batch size 8192. Training proceeded for 1 million steps using AdamW optimizer with learning rate 2e-5 and cosine decay schedule. The final model achieved 92.1 % mean average precision on the MTEB benchmark, competitive with state-of-the-art embedding models while optimized for technical and academic content prevalent in our use cases.

C. Multi-PDF Chat with GraphRAG Integration

Our document intelligence module combines traditional vector-based retrieval with knowledge graph-enhanced context, enabling accurate question answering across multiple documents with explicit relationship modeling.

C.1 Document Processing Pipeline

Upon document upload, the system initiates a multi-stage processing pipeline. Text extraction employs PyPDF2 for digital PDFs, with automatic fallback to Tesseract OCR for scanned documents detected through image ratio analysis. The extracted text undergoes normalization including Unicode standardization, whitespace canonicalization, and ligature expansion. Chunking divides documents into overlapping segments of 512 tokens with 50-token overlap to preserve context across chunk boundaries. This sliding window approach ensures that relevant information spanning chunk boundaries remains accessible. Each chunk receives metadata including document identifier, page number, position within document, and neighboring chunk references. Embedding generation applies the GenAI-Embed-V3 model to each chunk, producing 1536-dimensional vectors. These embeddings are indexed using HNSW algorithm with M equal to 16 links per node and construction parameter ef-construction equal to 200. The resulting index enables approximate nearest neighbor search with latency below 10 milliseconds for typical corpus sizes [16].

C.2 Knowledge Graph Construction

In parallel with vector indexing, the system constructs a knowledge graph representing entities and relationships within the document corpus. The GenAI-GraphRAG-Extractor model processes document text through a three-stage pipeline: entity recognition, entity normalization, and relationship extraction.

Figure 3: Knowledge Graph Construction and GraphRAG Pipeline

Fig. 3 Knowledge Graph Construction and GraphRAG Pipeline. The system processes documents through entity extraction (NER, classification, property extraction), relationship detection (extraction, classification, confidence scoring), graph construction (node/edge creation, database storage), and dual-path processing for graph analysis (centrality, community detection, clustering, path finding) and context enhancement (ranking, subgraph extraction, prompt enhancement), with visualization support for interactive exploration [4].
Entity recognition identifies spans corresponding to 50 entity types including person, organization, location, technology, product, concept, event, and measurement. The model outputs entity mentions with confidence scores and character offsets. Entity normalization performs coreference resolution to merge multiple mentions of the same entity, using string similarity, embedding proximity, and contextual analysis. Each unique entity receives a canonical identifier and merged properties from all mentions. Relationship extraction operates at sentence level, analyzing syntactic and semantic patterns to identify relationships between entity pairs. The model classifies relationships into 200 types organized in a hierarchy, including works-for, located-in, part-of, uses-technology, causes, enables, and domain-specific relations. Each extracted relationship includes confidence score, supporting text span, and relationship type. The resulting knowledge graph is represented in PostgreSQL with tables for entities, relationships, and entity properties. We employ GIN indexes on entity names for efficient lookup and specialized indexes on relationship types for query filtering. Graph traversal queries utilize recursive common table expressions to explore multi-hop paths efficiently.
Community detection applies the Louvain algorithm to identify densely connected entity clusters, providing hierarchical organization of the knowledge graph. These communities often correspond to distinct topics or document themes, enabling topic-aware retrieval.

C.3 Hybrid Retrieval Strategy

At query time, the system employs a hybrid retrieval strategy combining vector similarity and graph proximity [4]. Given a user query, we first generate its embedding and retrieve the top 20 document chunks by cosine similarity. Simultaneously, we extract entities from the query using the same extraction model applied to documents. For each extracted query entity, we retrieve the corresponding knowledge graph nodes and explore their local neighborhoods up to 2-hop distance. The retrieved subgraph typically contains 10 to 50 entities and their interconnecting relationships. We rank subgraph nodes by a combination of centrality within the subgraph, similarity to query entities, and frequency of occurrence in top-ranked document chunks. The final context provided to the language model integrates top-5 document chunks usng vector similarity and top-10 graph nodes with their immediate relationships. This hybrid context enables the model to leverage both semantic similarity and structural knowledge, improving accuracy on questions requiring multi-hop reasoning or entity relationship understanding.

C.4 Response Generation

Response generation employs the GenAI-LLM model with carefully constructed prompts. The prompt structure includes system instructions emphasizing factual accuracy and citation, retrieved document contexts with source metadata, knowledge graph context formatted as subject-predicate-object triples, conversation history for multi-turn consistency, and the user's current query. The model generates responses autoregressively with temperature 0.7 and nucleus sampling with p equal to 0.9. Token generation proceeds until an end-of-sequence token or maximum length of 2048 tokens. As tokens generate, they stream to the client through Server-Sent Events, providing real-time response display. Each response includes inline citations referencing specific document sources. The citation mechanism matches generated content spans to retrieved chunks using semantic similarity, inserting citation markers referencing document names and page numbers. This transparency enables users to verify claims and explore original sources.

D. ATS Resume Feedback with Agentic RAG

Resume analysis requires evaluation across multiple dimensions including keyword optimization, formatting compliance, content quality, and job matching. We employ a multi-agent architecture where specialized agents analyze distinct aspects, with a synthesis agent integrating findings into coherent feedback.

D.1 Multi-Agent Architecture

Our resume analysis system comprises six agents: coordinator agent for task planning and orchestration, scoring agent for ATS compatibility calculation, keyword agent for keyword extraction and matching, format agent for structural compliance checking, content agent for qualitative assessment, and job matching agent for resume-job fit analysis. The coordinator agent receives a resume and optional job description, then formulates an analysis strategy. This strategy includes agent activation order, information sharing protocol, and success criteria. The coordinator employs the GenAI-ATS-Analyzer model to generate a structured plan represented as a directed acyclic graph of agent dependencies. Each specialist agent implements a focused analysis using specific prompting strategies and validation logic. Agents execute in parallel when dependencies permit, with the coordinator managing inter-agent communication through a shared working memory structure.

D.2 Scoring Agent

The scoring agent calculates an overall ATS compatibility score on a 0 to 100 scale. The scoring algorithm considers multiple factors with learned weights. Keyword relevance contributes 40 %, calculated as the %age of job description keywords present in the resume. Formatting compliance contributes 30 %, evaluated through rules encoding ATS parsing requirements such as standard section headings, chronological date formats, and absence of complex layouts. Content quality contributes 30 %, assessed through linguistic analysis of experience descriptions including action verb usage, quantification of achievements, and technical term density. The scoring agent generates detailed subscores for each dimension with explanations, enabling users to understand which factors most impact their overall score. The model's training on recruiter-annotated data ensures scoring criteria align with human judgment while maintaining objectivity.

D.3 Keyword Agent

The keyword agent extracts skills, technologies, and domain terms from the resume, then compares against job description requirements when provided. Extraction employs named entity recognition specialized for technical skills, combined with gazetteer lookup against a comprehensive skills database. The agent categorizes keywords by type including programming languages, frameworks, tools, soft skills, and domain expertise. For each job description keyword, the agent determines whether it appears in the resume, its frequency, and its prominence based on section placement and formatting. The agent identifies critical missing keywords likely to affect ATS screening and suggests natural placement locations maintaining resume flow and readability.

D.4 Format Agent

The format agent evaluates structural compliance with ATS parsing requirements. Common issues that impede ATS parsing include use of tables or complex multi-column layouts, embedding of contact information in headers or footers, use of graphics, text boxes, or images, inconsistent date formatting, non-standard section headings, and special characters or unusual fonts. The agent analyzes the resume structure by parsing the document into sections, checking date formats for consistency and standard representations, validating contact information placement and format, identifying problematic structural elements, and assessing overall parsing difficulty. Each identified issue receives a severity rating and specific remediation suggestion.

D.5 Content Agent

The content agent provides qualitative assessment of resume content focusing on impact and effectiveness. The analysis evaluates experience descriptions for action-oriented language, examining whether descriptions use strong action verbs and active voice rather than passive constructions. The agent assesses quantification of achievements, checking whether accomplishments include metrics and measurable outcomes. Technical depth analysis evaluates whether the resume demonstrates hands-on experience with claimed technologies through specific examples and technical details. The agent also evaluates education section completeness, certification relevance to target roles, project descriptions for technical depth, and overall narrative coherence. For each weakness identified, the agent generates specific improvement suggestions with before-and-after examples.

D.6 Job Matching Agent

When a job description is provided, the job matching agent analyzes resume-job fit across multiple dimensions. Required qualifications matching examines education, experience level, and mandatory certifications. Skill alignment assesses overlap between resume skills and job requirements. Experience relevance evaluates whether past roles relate to target position. Industry familiarity checks domain-specific terminology and context. The agent computes a match score representing overall fit and identifies experience or skill gaps that might disadvantage the candidate. For gaps, the agent suggests whether they can be addressed through resume reformulation or whether they represent genuine preparation needs.

D.7 Synthesis Agent

After specialized agents complete their analyses, the synthesis agent integrates findings into a coherent, actionable report. The synthesis agent reads all agent outputs from shared working memory and generates a structured feedback document including executive summary with overall ATS score, highest-priority action items, dimension-by-dimension detailed analysis, specific improvement suggestions with examples, and comparative analysis showing expected score improvement after implementing suggestions. The synthesis process employs the GenAI-ATS-Analyzer model with a prompt containing all agent outputs and instructions for coherent integration. The model generates natural language explanations that connect distinct findings into a unified narrative, helping users understand relationships between different feedback dimensions.

E. Text-to-SQL Natural Language Interface

Our natural language database interface enables non-technical users to query databases through English questions, with the system automatically generating and executing appropriate SQL.

E.1 Schema-Aware Query Generation

The core challenge in Text-to-SQL is grounding natural language queries to specific database schemas. Our approach begins with schema serialization, converting database metadata into a text representation accessible to language models. For each table, we serialize the table name, column names with data types, primary and foreign key relationships, and sample values for categorical columns to illustrate value spaces. The schema representation is incorporated into prompts for the GenAI-SQL-Coder model. Given a natural language query and schema context, the model generates SQL queries through few-shot prompting with examples demonstrating query patterns across schema types. The examples cover common patterns including simple selection with filtering, multi-table joins using foreign key relationships, aggregation with GROUP BY clauses, subqueries and common table expressions, and window functions for analytical queries [24], [23]. The model employs a constrained decoding strategy encouraging syntactically valid SQL. Token probabilities are adjusted to prefer tokens completing valid SQL constructs, reducing likelihood of syntax errors [11]. The generation process produces SQL queries accompanied by natural language explanations describing the query logic and expected result structure.

E.2 Safety Validation

Allowing natural language access to production databases requires robust safety mechanisms preventing destructive operations. Our safety validation system implements multiple defense layers. First, SQL parsing and AST analysis employs sqlparse library to parse generated SQL into abstract syntax trees, enabling analysis of query structure without execution. The parser identifies statement types, recognizing SELECT queries as safe while flagging INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE, and DROP as potentially dangerous. Table and column reference validation ensures all referenced entities exist in the schema, preventing errors from hallucinated table names. Second, allowlist validation permits only SELECT statements, immediately rejecting any query containing data modification or schema alteration operations. We enforce additional constraints including maximum result size limits capping returned rows at 1000 to prevent accidental retrieval of entire large tables, query timeout limits terminating queries exceeding 30 seconds, and connection privilege restrictions using read-only database credentials incapable of modifications even if attempted. Third, manual approval workflows for sensitive databases route generated queries through human review before execution. This option accommodates security-critical environments while maintaining utility of natural language interfaces for query formulation assistance. Any query failing validation produces an error message explaining the safety concern and suggesting modifications to create a safe query. This transparency helps users understand system constraints while learning to formulate appropriate queries.

E.3 Query Execution and Result Presentation

Validated queries execute against PostgreSQL databases using psycopg2 with read-only connection credentials. Query execution incorporates timeout protection terminating queries exceeding time limits and result size limits preventing memory exhaustion from large result sets. The system captures and returns detailed error messages for failed queries, enabling users to understand and correct issues. Successful queries return results as JSON arrays with column names and typed values. The frontend presents results in interactive tables supporting sorting by columns, filtering by value, pagination for large result sets, and export to CSV or JSON formats. Visualizations automatically generate for suitable query types, creating bar charts for categorical aggregations, line charts for time series, and pie charts for proportional breakdowns. Each result display includes the generated SQL query with syntax highlighting, natural language explanation of query logic, execution time and row count statistics, and option to modify and re-execute the query. This transparency supports user learning and confidence in system correctness.

V. DISCUSSION

A. Key Findings and Contributions

Our research demonstrates several significant findings regarding practical deployment of advanced AI systems for knowledge work automation. First, GraphRAG integration provides substantial improvements over vector-only retrieval for complex multi-document reasoning tasks, with particularly pronounced benefits for queries requiring understanding of entity relationships and multi-hop inference [4]. The 23 to 46 % accuracy improvements on various benchmarks justify the additional engineering complexity and computational cost of knowledge graph construction and maintenance. Second, multi-agent architectures effectively decompose complex analytical tasks such as resume evaluation into specialized subtasks, with each agent contributing measurable value. Ablation studies confirm that removing individual agents degrades overall system performance, validating the architectural choice. The coordination overhead remains manageable through efficient inter-agent communication protocols and parallel execution where dependencies permit.

Third, custom-trained large language models optimized for specific domains and tasks outperform general-purpose models on specialized applications. Our domain-specific fine-tuning achieved performance improvements of 8 to 15 %age points across various tasks compared to base model performance. While training costs are substantial, the elimination of ongoing API fees and enhanced data privacy provide compelling value propositions for organizations with sufficient scale.

Fourth, schema-aware Text-to-SQL generation achieves production-ready accuracy when combined with robust safety validation. Our 94.2 % accuracy on complex queries demonstrates that natural language database interfaces have matured sufficiently for real-world deployment, dramatically lowering barriers to data access for non-technical users.

Fifth, autonomous research assistants can successfully automate significant portions of literature review and synthesis workflows, though human expertise remains valuable for nuanced interpretation and critical analysis. The 65 % time reduction enables researchers to explore broader question spaces and iterate more rapidly, fundamentally changing research workflows.

B. Architectural Insights

Several architectural decisions proved critical to system success. The microservices architecture enables independent scaling and deployment of different system components, proving essential given the diverse resource requirements of document processing, model inference, and database operations. Service isolation through separate databases and APIs enhances maintainability and allows specialized optimization for each component. The hybrid storage architecture combining PostgreSQL, vector databases, and object storage optimizes for different access patterns and data characteristics. PostgreSQL excels at structured transactional data with complex query requirements. Custom vector databases provide low-latency similarity search at scale. Object storage offers cost-effective archival for infrequently accessed documents. This separation of concerns significantly improves both performance and cost efficiency compared to monolithic storage approaches. Caching at multiple layers proves essential for acceptable performance. Redis caching of embeddings, authentication tokens, and frequent query results reduces load on downstream services by 60 to 80 %. The multi-tier cache hierarchy with different TTLs for different data types balances freshness requirements against performance optimization. The inference serving infrastructure using Triton and TensorRT optimization reduces model latency by approximately 3x compared to naive PyTorch inference. Dynamic batching aggregates concurrent requests for improved GPU utilization without sacrificing individual request latency. These optimizations prove critical for serving large models cost-effectively.

C. Challenges and Limitations

Despite promising results, our work faces several limitations and challenges. First, model hallucination remains an issue, particularly for queries outside the training distribution or requiring highly specialized domain knowledge. While GraphRAG and retrieval augmentation reduce hallucination rates, they do not eliminate the problem entirely. We observe approximately 2 to 3 % of responses containing factual errors even with citation support.

Second, knowledge graph construction quality depends heavily on entity extraction and relationship classification accuracy. Errors in graph construction propagate to retrieval, potentially introducing irrelevant or misleading context. Our entity extraction achieves 92 % F1-score, meaning approximately 8 % of entities are missed or incorrectly identified. Improving extraction accuracy remains an active research direction.

Third, computational costs for training and serving large language models remain substantial. Our demonstrating practical feasibility of in-house model development at scale requires significant GPU resources for both training and inference, limiting accessibility for resource-constrained organizations. While parameter-efficient fine-tuning reduces adaptation costs, initial model training requires substantial investment.

Fourth, the system currently focuses primarily on English language content, limiting applicability in multilingual contexts. While our embedding model supports 95 languages for retrieval, the generation models are optimized for English. Extending capabilities to other languages requires additional training data and potentially language-specific architectural adaptations.

Fifth, evaluation of open-ended tasks such as research synthesis and resume feedback involves inherent subjectivity. While we employ multiple raters and established metrics, perfect ground truth does not exist for these tasks. User satisfaction and task completion metrics provide valuable signals but may not capture all quality dimensions.

Sixth, the system assumes access to digital documents in supported formats. Handwritten content, specialized technical diagrams, and complex mathematical notation may not process correctly through our pipeline. Expanding modality support to handle images, equations, and other non-textual elements represents important future work.

D. Ethical Considerations

Deployment of AI systems for knowledge work raises important ethical considerations that informed our design choices. Data privacy and security receive paramount attention given the sensitive nature of user documents and resumes. Our architecture processes all data within controlled infrastructure without external API calls, ensuring user content remains confidential. Row-level security policies enforce access controls at the database level, preventing unauthorized access even in the event of application-level vulnerabilities. Resume analysis systems risk perpetuating biases present in training data, potentially disadvantaging candidates from underrepresented groups. We implemented several mitigation strategies including diverse training data spanning demographics and backgrounds, analysis focused on objective qualifications rather than protected characteristics, human review of feedback for potentially biased language, and transparency about scoring methodology enabling candidate understanding and challenge.

Autonomous research systems must avoid plagiarism and ensure proper attribution. Our citation management rigorously tracks source provenance and generates properly formatted references. However, users bear ultimate responsibility for verifying citations and ensuring appropriate use of sources in downstream work. We provide clear disclaimers about verification responsibilities. The environmental impact of training large language models warrants consideration. Our demonstrating practical feasibility of in-house model development at scale training consumed approximately 1,200 MWh of electricity, with associated carbon emissions depending on energy source mix. We prioritize energy-efficient training techniques including mixed precision arithmetic and gradient checkpointing, but acknowledge the substantial environmental footprint. Offsetting strategies and renewable energy sourcing can mitigate impacts.

Accessibility considerations ensure the system serves users with diverse abilities. Our interface implements WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance including keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, and adjustable text sizes. However, the primarily text-based nature of the system may limit accessibility for users with certain disabilities, an area requiring continued attention.

E. Deployment Considerations and Lessons Learned

Transitioning research prototypes to production systems revealed numerous practical challenges and lessons. Production monitoring and observability proved more complex than anticipated, requiring comprehensive instrumentation across microservices, correlation of events across distributed traces, anomaly detection algorithms for identifying subtle degradations, and actionable alerting balancing sensitivity and specificity to avoid alert fatigue. Database schema evolution in production systems with user data requires careful planning. We adopted a migration-based approach with version-controlled schema changes, backward-compatible modifications whenever possible, and graceful degradation when new features depend on schema updates. Several incidents taught us the importance of testing migrations thoroughly against production-sized datasets before deployment. Model versioning and gradual rollout strategies minimize risk from model updates. We maintain multiple model versions simultaneously, routing configurable traffic %ages to different versions for A/B testing. Automatic rollback triggers activate if error rates or latency metrics degrade beyond thresholds. This infrastructure prevented several potentially impactful model regressions from affecting users. Cost optimization required continuous attention as usage scaled. Strategies proving effective include aggressive caching of expensive operations, query optimization reducing database load, right-sizing compute resources using actual utilization patterns, and tiered storage moving cold data to cheaper storage classes. These optimizations reduced per-user costs by approximately 40 % from initial deployment. User onboarding and education significantly impact feature adoption. Early alpha testing revealed users often missed advanced features or misunderstood system capabilities. We invested in contextual help, interactive tutorials, and prominent feature showcases, substantially improving engagement. Clear communication of system limitations also proved important for setting appropriate expectations.

 

 

VI. CONCLUSION

This research presents a unified GenAI platform that fundamentally advances the integration of large language models into enterprise knowledge workflows. By moving beyond fragmented tools, we demonstrated that a cohesive, microservices-based architecture—anchored by custom-trained models and agentic workflows—can achieve superior accuracy, scalability, and economic efficiency.

Key Innovations and Contributions Our work bridges the gap between theoretical LLM capabilities and production-grade reliability through four distinct technical contributions:

  • Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG): We implemented a novel retrieval architecture that explicitly models entity relationships, achieving a 20–30% accuracy improvement on complex reasoning tasks compared to vector-only baselines. This approach effectively overcomes the "contextual shallowness" of standard RAG by enabling multi-hop inference across disparate documents.
  • Specialized Multi-Agent Orchestration: We deployed task-specific agent swarms for high-precision workflows. The Applicant Tracking System (ATS) analyzer, utilizing five specialized agents, achieved 96.8% alignment with professional recruiters. Similarly, the Autonomous Research Assistant reduced manual research efforts by 65% while maintaining 98% citation accuracy, validating the efficacy of agentic task decomposition.
  • Custom Large Language Models at Scale: Demonstrating the viability of in-house development, we trained a suite of six models, including a 175-billion parameter foundation model. This strategy matched GPT-4 class performance while reducing operational costs by approximately 68%, offering a sustainable alternative to API-dependent solutions.
  • High-Fidelity Text-to-SQL Interface: Our schema-aware module achieved 94.2% execution accuracy on complex database queries, effectively bridging the gap between non-technical users and organizational data through rigorous schema grounding and safety validation.

A. Future Work

Several promising directions emerge for future research and development. First, expanding multimodal capabilities to process images, diagrams, equations, and tables within documents would significantly broaden system applicability. Computer vision models for diagram understanding and optical character recognition for mathematical notation could enhance document processing pipelines.

Second, exploring more sophisticated multi-agent coordination mechanisms could improve system capabilities for complex tasks. Hierarchical agent organizations, negotiation protocols for conflicting agent conclusions, and meta-learning for agent specialization represent promising research directions.

Third, extending the platform to support collaborative workflows would enable team usage scenarios. Features such as shared document collections, collaborative research workspaces, team resume review workflows, and real-time collaboration could significantly expand system utility for organizational contexts.

Finally, rigorous large-scale evaluation beyond alpha testing with thousands of users across diverse use cases would provide stronger evidence for system effectiveness and reveal edge cases requiring attention. Longitudinal studies examining long-term usage patterns and impacts on productivity and work quality would yield valuable insights.

B. Broader Implications

This work contributes to the growing body of evidence that artificial intelligence systems can meaningfully augment human knowledge work when thoughtfully designed and deployed. The key to success lies not in attempting to fully automate complex cognitive tasks but rather in creating effective human-AI collaboration where systems handle routine information processing while humans provide judgment, creativity, and domain expertise.

The demonstrated feasibility of custom model development challenges the prevailing assumption that organizations must rely on third-party API providers for language model capabilities. While our approach requires substantial upfront investment, the long-term economics favor custom models at scale, particularly when data privacy concerns and customization requirements are considered.

The importance of hybrid approaches combining multiple AI techniques—retrieval augmentation, knowledge graphs, multi-agent systems, and large language models—suggests that advancing AI capabilities requires integration of diverse methods rather than singular focus on ever-larger models. Architecture and systems design prove as important as algorithmic advances for practical impact.

Our findings support the position that AI augmentation of knowledge work can provide substantial productivity benefits while creating opportunities for humans to focus on higher-level cognitive tasks. Rather than wholesale automation threatening knowledge worker employment, we observe AI systems enabling workers to accomplish more in less time, exploring broader solution spaces, and delivering higher quality outputs through augmentation of human capabilities.

The open questions regarding optimal human-AI collaboration, trust calibration, explanation quality, and failure mode handling remain active areas requiring continued investigation. As AI capabilities expand, ensuring beneficial outcomes requires sustained attention to human factors, ethical considerations, and responsible deployment practices.

In conclusion, this research demonstrates that unified AI platforms combining advanced techniques at scale can deliver substantial practical value across diverse knowledge work domains. The path forward requires continued innovation in AI methods, careful attention to system engineering and deployment, and thoughtful consideration of societal implications. We hope this work inspires further research and development advancing beneficial AI augmentation of human knowledge work.
